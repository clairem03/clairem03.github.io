The why

Taking something from zero to one

Buying beef directly from a local farm is better for the buyer and fairer to the farmer — and oddly hard to actually do. I co-founded Farm to Table to make that path approachable, and to find out whether I could take a product from a blank page to something real.

It became the project where I learned what the early, messy part of building actually asks of you: deciding what to make, what to leave out, and how to reach an MVP without drowning in everything it could eventually become.

My role

Co-founder, across the whole arc of the work

I ran the user research that decided what we built, set the roadmap and the MVP scope, translated what I learned into user flows and wireframes, and built the frontend that turned the concept into something people could open and browse. I worked alongside a small engineering team, aligning on constraints and iterating as we went.

The value I added wasn't any single screen — it was the judgment about scope: what genuinely mattered for a first version, and what was a distraction dressed up as a feature.

A decision I'm proud of

Knowing what not to build

The call I'm proudest of was leaving online checkout out entirely. A beef share is a high-trust, high-consideration purchase that closes on the farmer's terms — so I scoped Farm to Table as a directory and reservation-inquiry tool, not a marketplace. Its job is to help a buyer trust a farmer enough to reach out, not to process a sale.

That restraint kept the MVP pointed at the one thing worth proving first: that people would actually browse, compare, and make contact.

The MVP

What it became

The result is a directory where buyers find the farms serving their state and review what each one publishes — built so the unfamiliar parts of buying a beef share feel approachable rather than intimidating.
